Welcome to the mini digital edition of Mary Shelley's Frankenstein. Made as a project for Text as Data II, taught at the University of Antwerp.

The origin of the coming into being of Frankenstein started in the summer of 1816. The then 20 year old Mary Shelley and her husband Percy Shelly joined Lord Byron in Cologny, near Geneva. That summer they read ghost stories at the evenings, Byron proposed each of them to write a horror story.

Originally published anonymously as a three-volume novel on January 1, of just 500 copies by the publisher Lackington, Hughes, Harding, Mavor & Jones. Up to this date there has been an ongoing debate who the author of this famous work could be, Mary shelly or Percy Shelly. However Mary Shelly writes in 1831 in the introduction of a new edition of Frankenstein :“busied [herself] to think of a story, – a story to rival those which had excited us to this task. One which would speak to the mysterious fears of our nature, and awaken thrilling horror – one to make the reader dread to look round, to curdle the blood, and quicken the beatings of the heart”. Complicating things, Mary Shelley's own original story and drafts are lost. In collaboration with Percy Shelley they used two notebooks (A and B) whose disbound pages survive as Bodleian MS Abinger c.56 and c.57. used as a digitized source for this mini digital edition.

Before he was twenty-one, Shelley had been expelled from Oxford for promulgating his atheistic views, agitated for Irish independence in Dublin, and published political pamphlets (in both prose and verse) that gained him the attention of government spies. hey left England in March 1818 not long after the anonymous publication of Frankenstein. Shelley would spend the rest of his life in Italy, famously drowning in July 1822, while sailing during a storm.
The Digital Bodleian, launched in 2015, aims to unify the Bodleian Libraries' vast and diverse digitized collections into an accessible portal. It has become an important tool for research and teaching. The ten pages of Chapter 7 (folio 21r to 25v) in this mini digital edition can be found at the Bodleian Library MS. Abinger c. 56 resource. This digitized insight gives us a feel of the creative process of writing (more about this can be found Write,Cut,Rewrite exhibition at the Bodleian Libraries.
